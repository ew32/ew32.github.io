RBAC vs ABAC - Role-Based vs Attribute-Based Access Control

Posted on 2026-04-26 Edited on 2026-05-26 In Backend , Authentication
A comparison of the model differences, pros and cons, and practical patterns of RBAC and ABAC, plus a hybrid design that combines them.

The Evolution of Access Control Models

Access control is the mechanism that decides “who can access what,
under which conditions.” Early systems used an ACL (Access Control
List), but management became hard as the number of users grew. RBAC
solved that, and ABAC emerged when finer-grained control was needed.

RBAC - Role-Based Access Control

RBAC (Role-Based Access Control) doesn’t grant permissions directly
to users — it grants them indirectly through roles.

For example, you can model it like this.

Its strength is simplicity. “This user has the admin role, so they
can do everything” is intuitive, and admins can manipulate it easily
through a GUI.

Its weakness is role explosion. When permissions vary by
department, region, and project, roles like Sales-APAC-ReadOnly
and Sales-EMEA-Editor proliferate endlessly.

ABAC - Attribute-Based Access Control

ABAC (Attribute-Based Access Control) decides access with policies
that combine attributes of the Subject, Resource, Action, and
Environment.

Example policy: “A marketing department employee can update
campaigns owned by their own department only during business hours
(09:00–18:00).”

Expressed in OPA’s (Open Policy Agent) Rego:

ABAC’s strength is expressiveness. You can express dynamic
conditions as policies without predefining roles. Its weaknesses are
complexity and debugging difficulty — it’s hard to trace
which policy made which decision.

Comparison

Item RBAC ABAC
Permission unit Role Attribute combination
Policy expression Static, explicit Dynamic, conditional
Management tooling GUI-friendly Needs a policy language
Audit Easy Hard
Scalability Limited Very high
Adoption cost Low High

A Hybrid Approach

In practice, a RBAC + ABAC hybrid combining both models is
common.

Grant base permissions simply with RBAC, and handle fine,
data-level conditions (owner, confidentiality level, time of day)
with ABAC. AWS IAM is a representative hybrid model, using roles +
condition policies together.

ReBAC, Another Direction

Recently, ReBAC (Relationship-Based Access Control) has been
getting attention. Google’s Zanzibar paper was the starting point,
evaluating a relationship graph like “does user A belong to the
author group of document X?” Open-source implementations include
SpiceDB and OpenFGA.

Practical Recommendations

  • For a simple internal tool, start with RBAC.
  • For a multi-tenant SaaS, design RBAC + tenant-isolation ABAC conditions together.
  • If policies become scattered throughout the code, externalize them with a policy engine like OPA.
  • Log every access decision so post-hoc auditing is possible.

Wrapping Up

RBAC is simple and can be adopted quickly, but as the business
grows complex, you’ll need ABAC’s expressiveness. Combining the two
models appropriately and separating policy out of the code lowers
maintenance cost long-term.
